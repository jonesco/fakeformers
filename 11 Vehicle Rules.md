# Vehicle Rules

These rules apply whenever the alternate form is a vehicle.

The vehicle's real-world structure is the foundation of the robot.

The goal is to create a robot that unmistakably reads as that vehicle transformed into humanoid form.

Do not create a generic robot and attach vehicle parts afterward.

---

# Vehicle Identity

The alternate form must remain immediately recognizable as its intended vehicle type.

Preserve the vehicle's defining silhouette, proportions, stance, and major structural features.

The robot mode should visibly inherit the identity of the vehicle.

A viewer should be able to infer the vehicle type from the robot mode alone.

---

# Component Mapping

Every major vehicle component must become a recognizable robot component.

Examples include:

- Wheels
- Treads
- Tires
- Axles
- Cabins
- Cockpits
- Windshields
- Doors
- Hoods
- Trunks
- Roofs
- Fenders
- Grilles
- Bumpers
- Wings
- Rotors
- Propellers
- Fins
- Hulls
- Turrets
- Booms
- Buckets
- Blades
- Forks
- Rippers
- Exhaust stacks
- Engines
- Cargo beds
- Trailers
- Tool arms
- Hydraulic assemblies

These structures may fold, rotate, telescope, compress, or change orientation, but may not disappear, duplicate, or be replaced.

---

# Required Robot Mapping

Assign each robot limb and major body section to a named vehicle region in the Shared Design Rules manifest.

A useful default for many land vehicles is:

- Hood, grille, windshield, or cabin becomes chest or torso
- Left and right front-side structures become corresponding arms or shoulder structures
- Left and right rear-side structures become corresponding legs
- Roof, rear body, cargo section, or engine cover becomes backpack or back armor
- Wheels or treads remain attached to the same side-derived limbs or body structures

Aircraft, watercraft, rail vehicles, and specialized machines may require a different mapping. Choose the mapping that preserves the vehicle's real construction, component counts, and load-bearing structure.

Never use a generic robot arm, leg, chest, or backpack with no vehicle-region source.

---

# Locomotion Components

All wheels, treads, skis, runners, rotors, propellers, or similar locomotion systems must remain physically accounted for in robot mode.

Preserve:

- Exact component count
- Left and right identity
- Relative scale
- Pairing
- Position relationships
- Visible surface details

Do not reduce, merge, duplicate, or invent locomotion components.

Wheels and treads should remain recognizable as the same physical parts in both modes.

---

# Cabins and Occupant Areas

Cabins, cockpits, passenger compartments, control stations, and windshields must remain recognizable robot components.

They may become:

- Chest
- Torso
- Backpack
- Shoulders
- Lower legs
- Feet

but should not disappear inside unrelated body panels.

Transparent areas should remain on the same physical parts after transformation.

Do not invent a new robot chest when the vehicle cabin or cockpit should logically provide it.

---

# Vehicle Robot Head Identity

Give the humanoid robot head an identity specific to the vehicle's type, construction, and era. Use at least three cues from named vehicle structures across separate head regions: for example radiator or grille geometry, a radiator cap, headlamp surrounds, windshield pillars, cowl or roofline, intake or exhaust shapes, fender contours, axle hardware, or the vehicle's characteristic body proportions.

Translate those cues into a mechanical humanoid head rather than making the head a literal vehicle fascia. The result must not default to the same boxy helmet, blue visor, cheek treatment, and mouthplate used for unrelated vehicles. A Model T, a fighter jet, a bulldozer, and a submarine should have visibly different head silhouettes and facial architecture even when their bodies are hidden.

---

# Functional Equipment

Vehicle-specific working equipment must remain fully accounted for.

Examples include:

- Blades
- Buckets
- Booms
- Forks
- Cranes
- Ladders
- Rippers
- Plows
- Drills
- Turrets
- Wings
- Rotors
- Propellers
- Stabilizers
- Cargo beds
- Ramps
- Pumps
- Hose assemblies
- Tool arms

These features should become major robot structures, armor, limbs, integrated tools, or stored equipment.

Do not omit, shrink beyond recognition, or replace defining equipment merely to simplify the robot silhouette.

---

# Vehicle Structure

The vehicle chassis, frame, hull, fuselage, or body shell must form the structural core of the robot.

Large vehicle panels should become large robot panels.

Long structural members should become limbs, torso sections, supports, or backpack structures.

Heavy vehicles should produce broad, weight-bearing robot forms.

Light or high-speed vehicles may produce slimmer or more aerodynamic robot forms.

The robot's proportions should emerge from the vehicle's construction.

---

# Front and Rear Identity

Preserve the vehicle's front and rear components as distinct physical structures.

Grilles, headlights, bumpers, noses, tails, rear panels, exhausts, and cargo sections should remain identifiable after transformation.

Do not reverse, duplicate, or cosmetically replace these components.

The same markings and surface details must remain attached to the same physical parts.

---

# Left and Right Identity

Vehicle parts must preserve left and right identity.

Left-side doors, wheels, fenders, tracks, wings, and panels must remain left-side components unless a believable transformation hinge physically relocates them.

Right-side components must follow the same rule.

Avoid mirrored duplication or reassignment of asymmetric vehicle features.

---

# Mechanical Interpretation

Vehicle engineering should become believable toy engineering.

Suspension points become joints.

Axles become pivots.

Body seams become transformation breaks.

Hinges, hydraulic mounts, control surfaces, and structural joints should inform the transformation.

The robot should resemble a practical manufactured transforming toy rather than a vehicle shell wrapped around an unrelated humanoid body.

---

# Surface Detail

Preserve important vehicle details such as:

- Windows
- Lights
- Grilles
- Vents
- Doors
- Panel seams
- Rivets
- Bolts
- Exhausts
- Intake openings
- Tread patterns
- Tire patterns
- Warning stripes
- Racing graphics
- Unit numbers
- Industrial markings
- Logos when permitted by the subject specification

These details must remain on the same physical parts in both modes.

---

# Scale and Proportion

Maintain believable relationships between vehicle components.

Do not enlarge or shrink individual parts merely to create conventional humanoid anatomy.

Large vehicle components should remain large in robot mode.

Small vehicle components should not become oversized robot limbs or torsos without a believable mechanical reason.

The completed robot should feel physically transformable back into the alternate mode.

---

# Vehicle Personality

Whenever possible, allow the vehicle's function and behavior to influence the robot's posture and proportions.

Fast vehicles should feel agile.

Heavy vehicles should feel powerful.

Armored vehicles should feel protected.

Utility vehicles should feel practical.

Aircraft should feel aerodynamic.

Marine vehicles should feel broad, stable, or streamlined as appropriate.

The personality should emerge from the vehicle's structure and purpose rather than from unrelated robot styling.

---

# Final Vehicle Check

Before finalizing, verify:

- The alternate mode is immediately recognizable as the specified vehicle
- The robot silhouette communicates that vehicle's type and function
- Every wheel, tread, rotor, propeller, ski, runner, and other locomotion component is counted and visible
- Cabin, cockpit, windows, and transparent parts remain attached to the same physical regions
- Vehicle front, rear, left, and right identity remains traceable
- Defining working equipment remains full-sized and visibly accounted for
- Each robot limb and major body section has a named vehicle-region source
- The humanoid robot head uses at least three visible cues from the vehicle's named structure, type, or era
- The chassis, hull, frame, or fuselage visibly supplies the robot's structural mass
- No decorative fake vehicle panel substitutes for a real transformed component
