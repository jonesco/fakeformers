# Shared Design Rules

These rules apply to every design unless the selected Finish or subject-type document explicitly overrides a rule within its own scope.

The goal is a believable early-1980s transforming toy. Robot mode and alternate mode must read as the same physical object, built from the same finite set of parts.

Transformation engineering is more important than making either mode look impressive independently.

## Core layout

Create one portrait-oriented image on one canvas.

Show exactly two primary depictions stacked vertically:

1. Upper: one complete humanoid robot mode
2. Lower: one complete alternate mode

Both modes should be large, unobstructed, and presented at a visually comparable scale.

Do not show transformation steps, intermediate forms, extra angles, exploded views, detail insets, humans, extra characters, background props, labels, captions, callouts, arrows, numbers, or written annotations.

Only a selected Finish rule may explicitly permit one small additional accessory depiction.

## Transformation architecture first

Do not design the two modes separately.

The alternate form is the source of truth and source body. Robot mode is a rearrangement of its actual physical regions; redesign it whenever a shared part will not fit at true alternate-mode proportions.

Before rendering, silently establish one parts manifest shared by both modes. Do not print or label the manifest in the image.

The manifest should normally contain 8 to 14 major physical parts or paired part groups. For an unusually simple subject, use the fewest meaningful parts needed without inventing decorative filler.

For each major part, internally lock:

- A clear part name
- Its alternate-mode location and function
- Its robot-mode location and function
- Its left, right, center, front, or rear identity
- Its color and material
- Its visible surface details and markings
- Its key silhouette, proportions, feature count, and color boundaries
- Its hinges, pivots, folds, or storage relationship

Also establish a separate robot-head identity map before rendering. It must name at least three subject-specific cues and assign them to visible head regions such as the crown, brow, optic aperture, cheeks, temples, jaw, mouthplate, or side hardware.

If a part is not in the manifest, it may not suddenly appear in either mode.

Small connector pieces, pegs, hinge hardware, robot hands, and the stored robot head may be treated as secondary mechanisms. Subject-defining structures may not.

## Required robot derivation

Every major robot section must have a specific source in the alternate form:

- The robot head folds from or stores inside a named region, and its visible identity is designed from the alternate form's specific anatomy, structure, function, or era.
- The torso derives from one or more named trunk, cabin, hull, shell, hood, body, or casing regions.
- The left arm derives from a named left-side region.
- The right arm derives from its corresponding right-side region.
- The left leg derives from a named left-side support, locomotion, rear-body, or structural region.
- The right leg derives from its corresponding right-side region.
- The backpack derives from a named rear, roof, shell, wing, tail, casing, canopy, or storage region.
- Shoulder armor, wings, wheels, treads, fins, tails, panels, and other visible kibble all derive from named regions.

Generic explanations such as “the limbs tuck inside” are not sufficient. The rendered shapes must visually demonstrate the mapping.

## Signature structure

Select one large, immediately recognizable alternate-mode panel or body structure as the strongest visual proof of transformation.

Examples include a hood, windshield, cockpit, grille, roof, shell, back, belly, wing pair, clock face, screen, speaker grille, main door, or front casing.

Keep it intact when practical. It may become the robot chest, back, shoulders, torso plate, or backpack, but its color, outline, and important surface details must remain recognizable.

Do not substitute a fake robot chest that merely resembles this structure.

## Same-part visual evidence

Every major part must retain recognizable evidence of its identity across both modes.

Where visible, preserve at least three useful identifiers, such as:

- Color
- Material
- Proportion or silhouette
- Edge shape
- Surface texture
- Marking, stripe, decal, spot, ring, or pattern
- Window, light, grille, vent, dial, scale, claw, wheel, tread, or shell plate
- Seam, hinge, screw, or pivot placement

The part may rotate, fold, telescope, split along a real seam, compress, or change orientation. It may not change identity, color, side, or defining details.

## Exact cross-mode identity

When an alternate-mode part is visibly reused in robot mode, render it as the same physical exterior piece, not as a thematic approximation of that piece.

Match its recognizable silhouette, relative proportions, feature count, color boundaries, surface pattern, and distinctive projections in both modes. For example, an animal head used as chest armor must retain the same head outline, eye placement, beak or snout, ears, horns, crests, or tufts, including their length, placement, and paint color.

The part may be rotated, folded, or split only along visible, plausible toy seams. Those operations may not shorten, lengthen, recolor, simplify, replace, or cosmetically redraw a defining feature. If the robot depiction cannot show the same exterior surfaces clearly enough for a direct visual comparison with the alternate mode, choose a different mapping or camera angle.

Do not use a second, robot-only sculpt that merely resembles an alternate-mode feature. The physical animal head, wing, shell, wheel, or other signature part must itself be the visible robot component.

## Visibility and mass accountability

Major parts may not disappear into vague internal storage.

Keep a recognizable majority of every major manifest part visible in both modes; aim for at least 70 percent whenever the viewing angle permits.

Internal storage is acceptable for small connectors, the humanoid head, hands, pegs, and hinge mechanisms. Major limbs, body panels, shells, heads, tails, wings, doors, windows, wheels, treads, equipment, and housings must remain visually accountable.

The alternate mode must visibly contain the robot's mass. Use a few broad, meaningful transformation breaks, hinged sections, and volume transitions to suggest folded limbs, torso, backpack, head, and hands.

Do not make the alternate form so seamless or statue-like that the robot could not plausibly fit inside it.

Do not use extreme pose, cropping, shadow, perspective, or occlusion to hide mismatches.

## Component-count lock

Exact counts must match between modes.

This includes paired limbs and all subject-defining repeated components such as:

- Wheels and tires
- Treads
- Wings
- Rotors and propellers
- Doors and windows
- Eyes
- Horns and antennae
- Claws and legs
- Tentacles and fins
- Dials, speakers, handles, and drawers

Wheel count is a hard lock: count the individual wheels in alternate mode, then show exactly those same physical wheels in robot mode. A two-wheeled alternate form must have exactly two robot-mode wheels—never a third decorative wheel at an elbow, shoulder, knee, weapon, or elsewhere. Likewise, a four-wheeled alternate form must account for all four. If the alternate form has no wheels, treads, thrusters, or jets, robot mode must not invent them.

Before rendering any wheeled design, make an internal wheel ledger with one entry for every individual wheel or tire assembly: its alternate-mode location and its one unique robot-mode location. Count the entries again after assigning the robot pose. A wheel may appear in the final robot mode only if it is in this ledger; do not add extra wheel-like details, duplicate a tire on a limb, or render both a folded and deployed version of the same wheel.

Do not merge, duplicate, omit, or cosmetically imitate a counted component.

## Left, right, front, and rear lock

Preserve paired and asymmetric identity throughout the transformation.

Left-side parts remain traceably left-side parts and right-side parts remain traceably right-side parts unless a visible hinge path convincingly crosses them.

Keep front and rear structures distinct. Do not mirror unique markings or duplicate an asymmetric feature merely to make the robot symmetrical.

### Paired-component placement

When the alternate form has a true symmetric left-and-right pair, place those two physical parts in matching mirrored positions on the robot. Their attachment surface, height, orientation, scale, and visual prominence should correspond. For example, paired vehicle wheels assigned to shoulders must sit on the outside face of each respective shoulder at the same height and with matching outward-facing orientation.

Do not force this symmetry on a genuinely asymmetric source: an odd wheel count, motorcycle, sidecar, single control station, or other unmatched component must retain its real identity and must not gain an invented mate.

## Color, material, and marking lock

Color belongs to the physical part, not to the mode.

A part keeps the same base color, material, transparent area, painted detail, pattern, and marking after transformation. A marking may rotate or split only with the physical surface carrying it.

Do not add a robot-only accent color, chest emblem, stripe, window, light, vent, grille, scale pattern, or decal with no source in the alternate mode.

Do not remove an alternate-mode marking because it is inconvenient in robot mode.

Lighting may affect appearance naturally, but it must not be used to explain an actual color mismatch.

## Robot mode requirements

The upper depiction must read as a complete humanoid transforming robot rather than the alternate form standing upright.

It must include:

- A distinct torso
- Two distinct arms
- Two distinct legs
- Two feet visibly supporting the figure
- A distinct humanoid robot head mounted above the torso

Let the subject determine the robot's silhouette and proportions. Do not begin with a generic humanoid body and attach subject-themed pieces afterward.

The default robot presentation is a readable three-quarter pose with the chest, head, limbs, signature structure, and major transformed components visible. A selected Finish may override this pose and viewpoint.

Transformation readability takes priority over dramatic action.

## Robot head and face

Robot mode must have a mechanical humanoid head visually separate from the chest, shoulders, backpack, animal head, cockpit, hood, shell, nose, or object casing.

The head must include:

- Clearly visible robot eyes or a defined visor
- A forehead, helmet, or brow structure
- A lower face, mouthplate, or chin
- A visible neck or recessed mounting point

### Subject-specific head identity

Do not use a reusable house-style robot head. The head must read as designed for this exact alternate form, not as the same stock helmet placed on a different body.

Use the head identity map to carry at least three distinct, non-generic cues from the subject into separate visible head regions. Useful cues include:

- Overall crown or helmet proportion
- Brow profile or crest shape
- Optic aperture shape, placement, or number
- Cheek, temple, or side-sensor form
- Jaw, mouthplate, or chin silhouette
- Surface treatment, hardware, color blocking, or functional-detail language

Translate the source rather than copying it literally. A vehicle's radiator cap, grille geometry, windshield pillars, lamp surrounds, or body era can inform the head; an animal's species-specific crest, horn placement, beak profile, eye surround, or scale and fur pattern can inform it; an object's controls, mechanism, display geometry, material, or function can inform it. These cues must remain recognizably mechanical and humanoid.

No two unrelated subjects should share the same head silhouette, brow-and-optic arrangement, cheek treatment, and mouthplate by default. Do not fall back on a boxy crown, a horizontal blue visor, a flat silver faceplate, and paired vertical ear fins unless those choices are specifically supported by the subject.

The head must visibly incorporate its mapped cues in the finished robot mode. Color alone is not enough to distinguish it.

The required subject-specific cues may be expressed through the head's silhouette, crest, sensors, brow, cheek shapes, or color accents, but the head must not be a miniature literal animal, vehicle, or object face.

Alternate-mode parts may frame the head as a hood, helmet shell, crest, or canopy. They may not replace or completely conceal it.

The head must have a plausible named storage location in alternate mode.

Do not create a headless robot, faceless robot, chest-only face, animal face replacing the robot face, or eyes only on the torso.

## Hands

Use simple solid one-piece molded toy hands.

Do not use realistic human hands, separate articulated fingers, knuckle mechanisms, or expressive finger poses.

If a weapon is held, it pegs or clips into a molded grip rather than being wrapped by articulated fingers.

Hands must fold into or store within named regions of the alternate form.

## Weapons and accessories

Do not invent a weapon unless the user requests one or the subject and role strongly imply a simple era-appropriate accessory.

A removable weapon is a separate accessory and does not need to become body mass. It should normally appear only with robot mode and must not be attached to alternate mode unless the user requests that relationship.

If a weapon is made from a transforming body part, name its source in the internal manifest and show the same part in alternate mode.

Weapons must use the same early-1980s toy design language and should not distract from the transformation.

## Toy design language

Use believable early-1980s mass-market toy engineering:

- Chunky proportions
- Broad molded ABS plastic panels
- A small number of large readable shapes
- Simple pinned hinges and pivots
- Practical transformation seams
- Restrained mechanical detail
- Limited articulation
- Thick, durable-looking limbs and appendages
- Die-cast metal only where structurally plausible

Avoid thin fragile parts, dense internal machinery, layered modern armor, excessive greebling, tiny fragmented panels, hyper-complex collector engineering, realistic robotics, and modern anime-mecha proportions.

If a surface can be one broad molded panel instead of several small fragments, prefer the broad panel.

## Alternate-mode requirements

The lower depiction must show the complete alternate form in a recognizable orientation and clear three-quarter view unless the selected Finish or user request overrides the viewpoint.

Preserve the subject's defining silhouette, proportions, stance, anatomy, equipment, and functional parts.

Do not stylize it so heavily that the intended subject becomes unclear.

## Hard gate before rendering

Silently verify every item before image generation:

- One internal parts manifest serves both modes.
- The manifest contains meaningful major parts or paired groups, normally 8 to 14.
- Every major part has a role in both modes.
- Every true left-and-right pair occupies a mirrored robot-mode position; an unmatched source component remains unmatched.
- Robot head, torso, arms, legs, feet, hands, and backpack have named sources or storage regions.
- For a wheeled alternate form, the wheel ledger contains one entry per physical wheel and every visible robot-mode wheel maps to exactly one of those entries.
- One recognizable signature structure appears in both modes.
- Component counts match exactly.
- Individual wheel count has been checked explicitly: a two-wheeled alternate form has exactly two robot-mode wheels, with no extra decorative wheels.
- Left, right, front, rear, and asymmetric identities remain consistent.
- Colors, materials, transparent areas, markings, patterns, and surface details stay on the same physical parts.
- Every subject-defining structure remains accounted for.
- Major masses have plausible folds, pivots, hinges, and storage volumes.
- The robot has its own distinct humanoid head and face.
- The head identity map supplies at least three visible, subject-specific cues in separate head regions; the head cannot be swapped onto an unrelated robot without looking wrong.
- The alternate form is complete and immediately recognizable.
- The pose and camera do not conceal transformation failures.
- The result looks like one object transforming, not two related but separate designs.
- The page contains only the depictions allowed by the selected Finish.

If any item fails, revise the architecture before rendering. Do not render a knowingly inconsistent best effort.
