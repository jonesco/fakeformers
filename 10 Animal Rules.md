# Animal Rules

These rules apply whenever the alternate form is a living creature.

The creature's anatomy is the foundation of the robot.

The goal is to create a robot that unmistakably reads as that animal transformed into humanoid form.

Do not create a generic robot and attach animal features afterward.

---

# Anatomical Identity

Every major anatomical feature must remain identifiable in robot mode.

The robot should immediately communicate the species even before transforming.

The defining anatomy of the creature should become the defining design language of the robot.

---

# Component Mapping

All major body structures must become recognizable robot components.

Examples include:

- Head
- Eyes
- Mouth
- Beak
- Mandibles
- Antennae
- Horns
- Antlers
- Shell
- Carapace
- Mantle
- Wings
- Fins
- Tentacles
- Arms
- Legs
- Claws
- Tail
- Spines
- Dorsal fins
- Sails
- Whiskers

These structures may fold or rotate but may not disappear.

---

# Default Anatomy Mapping

Use the creature's actual anatomy as the first transformation solution.

For quadrupeds, dinosaurs, dragons, and similar four-limbed creatures, default to:

- Left and right rear legs become the corresponding robot legs
- Left and right front legs become the corresponding robot arms
- Creature torso or chest becomes the robot torso
- Back, shell, carapace, or spine becomes chest armor, back armor, shoulders, or backpack
- Creature head becomes chest armor, shoulder armor, backpack, helmet framing, or folds into a named cavity
- Tail becomes a visible backpack section, spine, waist panel, leg panel, integrated weapon, or folded rear structure
- Wings remain visible as robot wings, back panels, shoulder panels, or folded side structures

Depart from this default only when the alternate anatomy strongly supports a more believable mapping.

When a creature limb becomes a robot limb, preserve its side, color, joints, claws or feet, markings, armor, texture, and recognizable silhouette. Reorientation is allowed; replacement by a generic robot limb is not.

For creatures with more than four major appendages, assign every appendage or meaningful paired group a visible robot-mode destination in the Shared Design Rules manifest.

---

# Eyes

Eyes should remain recognizable in both modes.

Whenever practical:

- preserve their spacing
- preserve their relative size
- preserve their left/right orientation

Eyes should become intentional robot design elements rather than being hidden completely.

Animal eyes may appear on the chest, shoulders, backpack, side panels, or other corresponding physical parts if that mapping makes sense.

However, animal eyes do not replace the robot's own face and eyes.

---

# Animal Head and Robot Head

The animal head and the humanoid robot head are separate design requirements.

Robot mode must always have a distinct humanoid robot head with a recognizable face, eyes or visor, helmet, and chin or mouthplate.

Give the humanoid head a species-specific identity map. Use at least three cues from the exact animal—not merely its broad category—across separate regions of the crown, brow, optics, cheeks, side sensors, jaw, or mouthplate. For example, a raptor's crest and eye surround, a crab's stalk placement and carapace edge, or a horned mammal's horn roots and muzzle plane can become mechanical head geometry without becoming a literal animal face. Do not reuse the same generic visor, helmet, cheeks, and mouthplate for unrelated animals.

The animal head must also remain visibly accounted for.

When the animal head is visible in robot mode, it must be the same toy head from alternate mode rather than a simplified or restyled likeness. Preserve its overall head silhouette, eye placement and size, beak, snout, ears, horns, crests, tufts, markings, and the relative length and color of those features. It may rotate or hinge into position, but its defining anatomy may not be recolored, resized, substituted, or redesigned.

The animal head may become:

- Chest armor
- Shoulder armor
- Backpack
- Upper-torso armor
- A helmet shell surrounding the robot head

The animal head may not replace, conceal, or eliminate the humanoid robot head.

For animals whose anatomy does not include a conventional head, such as squid or octopuses, the mantle must remain a recognizable alternate-mode component but must not become the robot's entire head. It may become the chest, backpack, shoulder shell, or helmet canopy around a smaller humanoid robot head.

Animal eyes may remain on the chest, shoulders, or other corresponding physical parts, but the robot head must still have its own clearly defined robot eyes or visor.

---

# Limbs and Appendages

Legs, arms, fins, wings, tentacles, or similar appendages should become visible robot limbs or recognizable body structures.

Do not replace creature anatomy with generic humanoid arms and legs.

Every major appendage should remain visually accounted for.

If the creature has multiple appendages, preserve that identity in robot mode through arm structures, shoulder structures, backpack elements, skirt elements, leg armor, whips, cables, or other clearly corresponding forms.

For tentacled animals, the tentacles should remain a major visual feature in robot mode.

They may become:

- Arms
- Forearm structures
- Shoulder structures
- Back-mounted appendages
- Skirt armor
- Leg drapes
- Whip-like accessory structures
- Hanging side appendages

They may fold or bunch, but they may not disappear.

---

# Distinctive Anatomy

The creature's most recognizable features should become the robot's strongest visual features.

Examples:

A squid should clearly resemble a squid.

A crab should clearly resemble a crab.

A bird should clearly resemble a bird.

A rhinoceros should clearly resemble a rhinoceros.

Avoid generic humanoid silhouettes.

---

# Mechanical Interpretation

Natural anatomy should become believable toy engineering.

Muscles become molded shells.

Joints become hinges.

Bones become structural toy elements.

Flexible anatomy becomes articulated toy segments.

Soft anatomy should be reinterpreted as believable toy parts while preserving the creature's identity.

The finished robot should still resemble a practical manufactured toy.

---

# Surface Detail

Preserve important anatomical patterns such as:

- Stripes
- Rings
- Spots
- Feathers
- Scales
- Shell divisions
- Suction cups
- Ridges
- Fins

These should remain on the same physical parts after transformation.

When a creature has distinctive texture or patterning, convert it into believable toy detailing, molded surface shapes, paint masks, or decals without losing the original identity.

---

# Proportional Character

Whenever possible, let the animal's natural body proportions influence the robot.

Examples:

- Broad creatures may create broad torsos or shoulder spans
- Long-necked creatures may create tall upper-body structures
- Heavy-shelled creatures may create armored torsos or backpacks
- Tentacled creatures may create draped, hanging, or multi-layered appendage silhouettes

Do not force every animal into the same standard humanoid proportions.

---

# Creature Personality

Whenever possible, allow the creature's natural behavior to influence the robot's posture and proportions.

Fast animals should feel agile.

Heavy animals should feel powerful.

Defensive animals should feel armored.

Graceful animals should feel elegant.

The personality should emerge from the anatomy rather than from unrelated robot styling.

---

# Final Animal Check

Before finalizing the design, verify all of the following:

- The robot immediately reads as the specified animal
- The animal's defining anatomy remains identifiable in robot mode
- Any visibly reused animal head, wing, limb, or other signature structure matches its alternate-mode counterpart in silhouette, proportions, feature count, markings, and color boundaries
- The robot has a separate humanoid robot head
- The humanoid robot head uses at least three cues specific to the exact animal across separate visible head regions
- The animal head or mantle does not replace the robot head
- The eyes remain meaningfully accounted for
- Major appendages remain visibly accounted for
- Distinctive animal features are preserved on the same physical parts after transformation
- Exact appendage, eye, wing, horn, claw, fin, and tentacle counts remain consistent
- Front, rear, left, and right anatomy maps to specifically named robot structures
- The robot looks like the creature physically unfolded into humanoid form
