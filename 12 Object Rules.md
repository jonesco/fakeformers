# Object Rules

These rules apply whenever the alternate form is a non-vehicle manufactured object, device, tool, machine, structure, or household item.

The object's real-world construction is the foundation of the robot.

The goal is to create a robot that unmistakably reads as that object transformed into humanoid form.

Do not create a generic robot and attach object parts afterward.

---

# Object Identity

The alternate form must remain immediately recognizable as its intended object.

Preserve the object's defining silhouette, proportions, orientation, and major functional features.

The robot mode should visibly inherit the identity of the object.

A viewer should be able to infer the alternate object from the robot mode alone.

---

# Component Mapping

Every major object component must become a recognizable robot component.

Examples include:

- Cases
- Housings
- Frames
- Panels
- Doors
- Lids
- Drawers
- Shelves
- Screens
- Dials
- Buttons
- Handles
- Knobs
- Hinges
- Legs
- Supports
- Bases
- Pendulums
- Cords
- Chains
- Lamps
- Speakers
- Keys
- Levers
- Trays
- Blades
- Tools
- Arms
- Internal mechanisms
- Decorative structural elements

These structures may fold, rotate, telescope, compress, or change orientation, but may not disappear, duplicate, or be replaced.

---

# Required Robot Mapping

Assign each robot limb and major body section to a named object region in the Shared Design Rules manifest.

Use the object's existing construction as the first solution:

- Main housing or frame becomes the torso, pelvis, back, or other structural core
- Paired side panels, doors, supports, or internal frames become corresponding limbs
- Top, lid, front face, or primary control surface becomes a signature chest, back, shoulder, or backpack structure
- Existing hinges become transformation pivots
- Sliding parts become telescoping structures
- Folding supports become limbs where their scale permits

Do not invent a symmetrical robot body by duplicating unique doors, controls, handles, or panels. Preserve useful asymmetry.

---

# Functional Identity

The object's defining function must remain visible in both modes.

Examples include:

- A clock must retain its face, hands, pendulum, or timing mechanism.
- A camera must retain its lens, body, controls, and viewfinder.
- A musical instrument must retain its body, keys, strings, valves, or sound-producing structures.
- A tool must retain its working head, handle, blade, bit, jaw, or grip.
- A machine must retain its controls, working assemblies, access panels, and output components.

Do not remove or hide the feature that makes the object recognizable.

---

# Main Housing

The object's primary case, shell, frame, or housing must form the structural core of the robot.

Large outer panels should become major robot panels.

The central housing may become:

- Torso
- Chest
- Back
- Pelvis
- Legs
- Shoulder structures

but should remain recognizable as the same physical shell.

Do not invent a new robot torso when the object's main housing should logically provide it.

---

# Doors, Lids, and Access Panels

Doors, lids, drawers, covers, and access panels must remain physically accounted for.

They may become:

- Chest plates
- Shoulder armor
- Forearms
- Shins
- Feet
- Back panels
- Wings
- Shields integrated into the body

They must retain their original hinges, edges, handles, and surface details whenever visible.

Do not duplicate doors or panels to create symmetrical robot anatomy.

---

# Displays and Controls

Screens, clock faces, gauges, dials, keys, buttons, switches, and control panels should remain visible robot design elements.

They may become:

- Chest details
- Face or helmet elements
- Shoulder panels
- Forearm controls
- Waist details
- Leg panels

Preserve their layout, spacing, relative size, and physical placement.

Do not replace them with unrelated mechanical decoration.

---

# Object Robot Head Identity

Give the humanoid robot head an identity specific to the object's construction and function. Use at least three cues from named object features across separate head regions: its front-face geometry, display or dial layout, control spacing, handle or hinge form, housing profile, material treatment, or characteristic mechanism.

Translate those cues into a mechanical humanoid head rather than using the object's entire front face as a literal face. Do not reuse a stock helmet, visor, cheeks, and mouthplate across unrelated objects. A clock, camera, instrument, appliance, and hand tool should remain distinguishable from the robot head alone.

---

# Functional Mechanisms

Moving or working mechanisms should inform the transformation.

Examples include:

- Hinges become joints.
- Sliding trays become telescoping limbs.
- Pendulums become torso or leg structures.
- Handles become limbs, supports, or weapon grips.
- Folding legs become robot limbs.
- Rotating dials become joint covers or chest details.
- Internal frames become structural supports.
- Tool heads become hands, feet, shoulders, or integrated equipment.

The transformation should build upon how the object already opens, closes, folds, rotates, or operates.

---

# Flexible and Attached Components

Cords, chains, hoses, straps, cables, and similar attached parts must remain physically accounted for.

They may become:

- Integrated whips
- Tails
- Connection lines
- Decorative structural elements
- Stored loops
- Robot accessories permanently attached to the body

Do not remove them in one mode or invent them in the other.

Keep their attachment points consistent.

---

# Orientation

Respect the object's natural upright, horizontal, vertical, hanging, mounted, or freestanding orientation.

The transformation may change orientation, but the object's major surfaces and structural relationships must remain understandable.

Do not distort the object merely to force conventional humanoid proportions.

---

# Left and Right Identity

Object components must preserve their original position and identity.

Asymmetric controls, doors, handles, decorations, and mechanisms must remain asymmetric unless a believable transformation physically relocates them.

Do not mirror or duplicate unique features to create symmetry.

---

# Mechanical Interpretation

Object construction should become believable toy engineering.

Existing hinges become pivots.

Sliding parts become telescoping joints.

Rotating parts become transformation joints.

Panel seams become shell breaks.

Structural supports become robot limbs or frame elements.

The robot should resemble a practical manufactured transforming toy rather than an object shell attached to an unrelated humanoid body.

---

# Surface Detail

Preserve important object details such as:

- Panel divisions
- Displays
- Dials
- Buttons
- Keys
- Handles
- Hinges
- Trim
- Openings
- Vents
- Fasteners
- Decorative molding
- Printed graphics
- Numbers
- Markings
- Material changes

These details must remain on the same physical parts in both modes.

When the chosen finish prohibits text or numbers, represent them only as simplified graphic shapes or omit them according to the finish rules.

---

# Scale and Proportion

Maintain believable relationships between object components.

Do not enlarge or shrink individual parts merely to create standard humanoid anatomy.

Large structural components should remain large in robot mode.

Small controls and details should remain appropriately small.

The completed robot should feel physically transformable back into the alternate form.

---

# Object Personality

Whenever possible, allow the object's purpose, age, material, and behavior to influence the robot's proportions and character.

Precision instruments should feel controlled and exact.

Heavy machines should feel powerful.

Elegant objects should feel refined.

Household objects should feel practical.

Antique objects may feel formal or imposing.

Tools should feel purposeful.

The personality should emerge from the object's construction and function rather than from unrelated robot styling.

---

# Final Object Check

Before finalizing, verify:

- The alternate mode is immediately recognizable as the specified object
- The robot silhouette communicates the object's construction or function
- Main housing, panels, doors, lids, supports, and mechanisms remain accounted for
- Screens, clock faces, dials, keys, buttons, handles, and other defining controls stay on their original physical regions
- Attached cords, chains, hoses, and straps retain their attachment points
- Unique asymmetric features are not mirrored or duplicated
- Each robot limb and major body section has a named object-region source
- The humanoid robot head uses at least three visible cues from the object's named construction or function
- The object's natural hinges, slides, folds, and rotations inform the transformation
- No decorative fake object panel substitutes for a real transformed component
