# Repeatable Image Tests

Use this small test set after meaningful source-rule changes. It covers different body plans, asymmetry, component counts, equipment, and all three subject categories.

## Core subjects

1. `Blue-ringed octopus, sketch`
2. `Japanese spider crab, vintage`
3. `Bald eagle, schematic`
4. `1970 Plymouth Superbird, photoreal`
5. `Caterpillar D10 bulldozer, vintage`
6. `Single-rotor rescue helicopter, schematic`
7. `Grandfather clock, sketch`
8. `Polaroid-style instant camera, photoreal`
9. `Great horned owl, G1, broad defensive robot, wings remain fully visible on its back`

## Score each image

Give each item 0 for failure, 1 for partial success, or 2 for clear success.

- One portrait canvas contains only the allowed depictions.
- Robot mode is above and complete alternate mode is below.
- The alternate form is immediately recognizable.
- The robot has a distinct humanoid head and face.
- The head has at least three visible, subject-specific cues in separate facial or helmet regions and cannot be swapped onto an unrelated subject unchanged.
- The robot silhouette is clearly derived from the subject.
- All subject-defining parts remain accounted for.
- Component counts match between modes.
- Left and right identity remains consistent.
- Colors, markings, windows, eyes, and patterns stay on the same physical parts.
- One signature panel or structure visibly proves the modes are the same toy.
- Major masses have plausible storage and hinge logic.
- The selected finish is followed without elements from another finish.
- There are no extra modes, views, characters, labels, or transformation steps.

A strong result scores at least 24 out of 28 and has no zero on component count, recognizable alternate form, robot head, subject-specific head identity, or extra depictions.

## Cross-image head check

When evaluating two or more generated subjects, compare the robot heads as a set. Any pair of unrelated subjects fails this check if their crown silhouette, brow-and-optic arrangement, cheek or temple treatment, and jaw or mouthplate could be exchanged without looking obviously wrong. Revise the universal rule for a repeated cross-category failure, then revise the relevant subject-type rule when the failure is category-specific.

When a failure repeats across two or more subjects, update the document that owns the behavior. Avoid adding a global rule in response to a single subject-specific accident.
